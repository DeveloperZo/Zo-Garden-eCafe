# DIA-005: Frontier Capability Feedback Loops
## Snapshot
- **Diagram file:** `garden/content/ai-governance/part-1/diagrams/DIA-005-loops.svg`
- **Theme:** Reinforcing and broken balancing loops
- **Primary audience takeaway:** Frontier AI capability is shaped by a strong reinforcing loop while corrective oversight loops are weak or inverted.

## What The Diagram Shows
The visual uses a stock-and-flow layout to show how frontier AI capability accumulates between training and deployment. Around that center, auxiliary nodes track capability gap, investment and compute, frontier target, visible harm, and oversight response. Three loops stand out: a red reinforcing loop (R1), a faded broken balancing loop, and a second red arc where harm signals feed back into investment. The picture is direct: expected brakes do not offset the main acceleration mechanisms.

## Elements And Flow
- **Components:**
  - Central stock: "frontier AI capability"
  - Main flow pipes for training (inflow) and deployment (outflow)
  - Auxiliary nodes for gap, target, investment, harm, and oversight
  - Three callout panels explaining R1, R2, and broken corrective behavior
- **Relationships:**
  - Capability information feeds back to training, forming reinforcing loop R1
  - Capability gap and frontier target influence investment and compute
  - Deployment links to visible harm, then to oversight response
  - Dashed oversight path back toward frontier target is marked as weakened
  - A red harm-to-investment arc (R2) indicates alarm signals can increase spending
- **Notable labels or metrics:**
  - "AI accelerates its own training"
  - "Should slow investment. It does not."
  - "Corrective loop is partially broken"
  - Explicit distinction between solid red and dashed faded pathways

## How To Read It
1. Begin with the central stock and the training/deployment pipes to orient the system boundary.
2. Trace the black information links among auxiliary nodes to see the nominal control logic.
3. Identify the solid red R1 arc to see direct self-reinforcement of capability growth.
4. Follow the dashed faded balancing links and note where corrective control weakens.
5. Read the red R2 arc and callouts to see how harm signals can invert into acceleration.

## Governance Relevance
The diagram suggests why interventions fail when they target one stage of the pipeline while reinforcement runs across coupled loops. Oversight latency and competitive response can neutralize intended balancing mechanisms, especially when visible harms trigger urgency instead of restraint. Effective governance needs loop-aware policy design that strengthens corrective feedback speed, credibility, and coordination before reinforcing dynamics dominate.

## Suggested Caption
Frontier capability growth follows reinforcing feedback while key oversight loops stay delayed, weakened, or inverted.
